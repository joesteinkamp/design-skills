# Slide Framework

Reference for structuring individual slides and managing presentation flow.

## Slide Anatomy

Every slide should have:
- **Title:** A declarative statement (not a topic label). "Users abandon checkout at step 3" not "Checkout Data."
- **Key point:** The one thing the audience should take away.
- **Evidence:** Supporting visual, data, quote, or example.
- **Speaker notes:** What you say that is not on the slide.

## Slide Types

### 1. Problem Slide
- Purpose: Establish urgency and relevance.
- Content: Problem statement + impact metric or user quote.
- Tip: Lead with the human impact, follow with the business impact.

### 2. Evidence Slide
- Purpose: Build credibility with data.
- Content: Key finding + supporting data visualization or quote.
- Tip: One finding per slide. Don't cram multiple data points.

### 3. Solution Slide
- Purpose: Present the design direction.
- Content: Design mock/wireframe + key design decisions annotated.
- Tip: Show the design, then explain it. Let the visual land first.

### 4. Comparison Slide
- Purpose: Show alternatives considered.
- Content: 2-3 options side by side with pros/cons.
- Tip: Present your recommendation last or highlight it visually.

### 5. Journey/Flow Slide
- Purpose: Show the user's experience.
- Content: Simplified journey or flow with pain points highlighted.
- Tip: Keep it high-level. Detail goes in appendix.

### 6. Ask Slide
- Purpose: Drive a decision.
- Content: Clear decision question + options + recommendation.
- Tip: Be explicit. "We need a decision on X by Y" not "Thoughts?"

### 7. Next Steps Slide
- Purpose: Create accountability.
- Content: Actions, owners, and dates.
- Tip: Keep it to 3-5 items. More than that and nothing gets done.

## Presentation Pacing

| Presentation Length | Slides | Structure |
|-------------------|--------|-----------|
| 5 min | 5-7 | Problem → Key Finding → Solution → Ask |
| 15 min | 8-12 | Problem → Context → Evidence → Solution → Alternatives → Ask → Next Steps |
| 30 min | 15-20 | Full narrative arc with deeper evidence and discussion time |
| 60 min | 20-25 | Full narrative + workshop/discussion segment |

## Audience-Specific Framing

### Executives (C-Suite)
- Lead with business impact and strategic alignment.
- Keep it high-level; detail in appendix.
- Focus on decisions and trade-offs.
- Time: aim for the shorter end of the range.

### Product Managers
- Lead with user problems and opportunity sizing.
- Show how design maps to product goals and metrics.
- Discuss scope and phasing.

### Engineering Leads
- Lead with feasibility and technical implications.
- Show interaction details and edge cases.
- Discuss implementation approach and dependencies.

### Cross-Functional Partners
- Lead with shared goals and collaboration points.
- Show where their input shaped the design.
- Highlight what you need from them.

## Common Presentation Anti-Patterns

- **The Reveal:** Building up to the design with too much preamble. Show the design early.
- **The Data Dump:** Showing every research finding. Curate the strongest 2-3.
- **The Apology Tour:** Over-explaining limitations. Acknowledge trade-offs, don't apologize.
- **The Feature Tour:** Walking through every screen. Focus on key decisions and rationale.
- **The Missing Ask:** Ending without a clear decision request. Always close with what you need.
- **The Monologue:** Not building in moments for questions or reactions. Pause after key points.

## Slide Writing Checklist

- [ ] Every slide has a declarative title (not a topic label)
- [ ] Each slide makes one key point
- [ ] Evidence supports claims (no unsupported assertions)
- [ ] Slides follow the narrative arc (problem → context → solution → ask)
- [ ] Speaker notes add context not visible on the slide
- [ ] Total slide count fits the time allocation
- [ ] The ask slide is explicit and actionable
- [ ] Appendix contains detail that supports but doesn't clutter the main deck
